Geology

The island's formation has been greatly influenced by major tectonic, magmatic and sedimentary events from the Paleozoic era and Mesozoic era to the Cenozoic era. Cliffs, caves, mountains… are all the result of these geological changes that have occurred in the past and that you are now able to observe in Sardinia.

Paleozoic era

In the Paleozoic era Sardinia was part of the Hercynian Chain, a mountain range that stretched across Europe. This era involved significant tectonic and metamorphic activities, including subduction, collision, and crustal thickening (Barca & Cherchi, 2002). Due to this movement of the land, magnificent coastal views and dramatic cliffs can be nowadays observed in Capo Spartivento, located in South Sardinia. This area was a result of the tectonic activity along the Gondwanan margin, which was a part of the Late Ordovician magmatic event during the Paleozoic era (Ludwig & Turi, 1989).

Mesozoic era

During the Mesozoic era, Sardinia went through marine transgressions during the Triassic and Jurassic periods, which can be observed in Nurra and Sulcis, where Jurassic limestone cliffs meet the azure sea (Barca & Cherchi, 2002).. In the region of Nurra you can visit Grotte di Nettuno, which shows the results of the marine transgressions during the Triassic and Jurassic periods. If you want to visit this geological site you will find more information on this website: Neptune's Grotto: official website of the City of Alghero.

Figure 2. Grotte di Nettuno (Photo Gallery · Grotta Di Nettuno, 2022)

Cenozoic era

The Cenozoic era has different epochs. A relevant epoch of the Cenozoic era is the Oligocene epoch, in which Sardinia separated from mainland Europe due to tectonic rifting, creating the Tyrrhenian Basin. This event also triggered volcanic activity, resulting in the island's current topography.

Later on, in the Miocene epoch there was volcanic activity that formed Monte Arci, where obsidian, a natural volcanic glass, is found. This area is not only geologically relevant but also historically important (Barca & Cherchi, 2002). If you are interested in ceramics and tools made of obsidian, you should check out the archeology section!

Places of interest
Montiferru

The Montiferru is an ancient volcanic complex that spans over five municipalities: Bonarcado, Cuglieri, Santu Lussurgiu, Scano di Montiferro and Seneghe. The shape is the result of lava flow that occurred millions of years ago and ‘formed’ basaltic landscapes such as the cliffs of the east and west coast plateaus including Abasanta. The highest peak of the massif is Mount Urtigu, 1050 metres high (Nuraxi, 2015). For more information on guided tours and other structures around the area visit this website: Montiferru’s Tour - Sardegna in Tour

Figure 4. Montiferru (The Montiferru Coast, Views from an Unexpected Sardinia. - Felix Hotels, 2023)

Capo Caccia

Capo Caccia is a promontory in North West Sardinia with high limestone cliffs formed from the Triassic to Cretaceous carbonates. It also forms part of the Marine Protected Area (MPA) of “Capo Caccia-Isola Piana”. Looking at the figure 5 you will be able to find 3 zones. Zone A is a restricted area that allows the preservation of biodiversity. However, in zones B and C you will be able to explore the island by guided boat tours, scuba diving, hiking and cliff viewing. You can check out this activities in this website: here

Mega Pillow in Masullas

A testament of early Miocene volcanic activity are pillow lavas, which are underwater lava formations. These lava formations are normally 1 to 1.5 meters in diameter. You can observe this phenomenon in Masullas, a town in the region of Marmilla. Here you can find a “Mega Pillow” which has an abnormally massive dimension of 12 metres long and 8 metres high!
